CollegePath: An end-to-end mobile application


Role
UX/UI Designer
Researcher
Project Duration
3 weeks (40 hrs/wk)
UX Academy Student Project
tools
Sketch, Whimsical, Miro, Invision

Overview


Applying to college is an exciting yet stressful time for high school seniors. I want to create a mobile app that provides students all the help they need when applying to college.


The Problem

For many high school seniors, the idea of heading off to college can be exciting. However, the application process to get there can be overwhelming, complicated and frustrating for students. Students need keep track of important deadlines, complete numerous application forms and essays, and make some hard decisions along the way.

Further, high school students are expected to do a lot of research on their own when navigating the college application process. While guidance counselors can and should be a resource for students, the nationwide average ratio of high school guidance counselors to students is 310:1. This can make it difficult for high school students to get the help they need when they need it.

In spite of this, many students seek guidance from their parents or other family members that have been through the college application process themselves. First-generation students, however, are the first in their families to apply to college and don’t have family members familiar with the postsecondary education system. Without experienced parents or other family members to help with the college application process, many first-generation students feel like they need to figure things out on their own. This can cause many first-generation students to feel overwhelmed by the college application process.

How might we make the college application process feel less overwhelming to students?

The Solution

  • A mobile app that acts as a resource hub for students to get help they need during the college application process.
  • When users open the app, they can look at their dashboard to get a tailored look at their college application process and timeline
  • In order to make the college application process feel a little less daunting, this app will be designed in a way that is inviting to high school students.

Design Thinking Process

* * * * *

Research


My main research goal is to develop an in-depth understanding of people’s experience with the college application process.

Questions I want to answer are:

  • What help is currently offered to high school seniors applying to college?
  • What pain points currently exist within the applying to college experience?
  • What makes the college application process not an enjoyable experience?
  • What do former college applicants wish they knew about the college application process?

Secondary Research

In November 2020, the Common Application, a tool used nationwide by students to apply to both public and private institutions, reported declines of 8 percent in the amount of applications submitted. Applications for first-generation students and those eligible for fee waivers are down 16 percent. Many theorize that this is because this group has been disproportionately impacted by COVID and its financial impact.

I also conducted research on potential features to include in my app. A feature I’m interested in adding to the CollegePath app is a timeline that shows users at a glance college application related tasks to complete throughout the year. I explored recommended college application timelines for high school seniors and found these examples: Example 1, Example 2, Example 3.


Competitive Analysis

I conducted a competitive analysis to determine what other apps are doing to help rising high school seniors throughout the college application process. It was somewhat difficult to find direct competitors because there are only a few apps with personalized college counseling as its primary focus. I realized that, due to the nuance behind every student’s college application, college counseling exists as an in-person service in either schools, non-profit organizations or as a private college counseling service.

I found two apps with elements of personalized college counseling: Pointer and College Prep. These apps help high school seniors find resources and keep track of important dates.

Both Pointer and College Prep use timelines and dashboards to help students keep up-to-date with where they are at in the college application process. However, both have pretty dated, hard-to-use interfaces.

Most college-centric apps I found focused on specific elements of the college application process--mostly scholarship search and college search. Because of this, I chose Scholly (a scholarship search app) and Niche (a college search app) as indirect competitors. Both Scholly and Niche have a strong user base and modern, easy-to-use UI. They also use dashboards where users can find information relevant to them the moment they open the app.



Surveys and User Interviews

My mobile app targets all college applicants, regardless of first-generation status. However, I recruited only first-generation students to take part in my surveys and interviews. This is because first-generation students face the most barriers when applying to college. I believe that by finding solutions for pain points experienced by this user base, my app will be as helpful as possible to all users.

I recruited 14 survey participants who all applied to college within the past 10 years. My survey participants ranged from ages 21-27 years old. For my user interviews, I recruited users who applied to college more recently. My user interview participants ranged from ages 18-20 years old.

My user interview participants consisted of:

  • one current college sophomore
  • two college freshmen
  • one high school senior who just completed the college application process

For both my surveys and user interviews, I asked participants to use three words to describe the college application process. This is a word cloud of the answers given by participants:


Some other notable findings were:

  • One key difference between findings from my surveys and user interviews was that participants in my user interviews used digital organizational tools to keep track of important dates and deadlines, including Excel, Google Docs, and Google Calendar. My survey participants either did not have a method to keep track of dates or used a planner to keep track of dates. While the younger users I interviewed were still just as stressed as the older survey participants were about keeping track of important dates, they all found digital organizational tools to be helpful.
  • In both my surveys and user interviews, I found that participants had significant trouble applying for financial aid. Most participants had to apply for financial aid on their own and found that to be stressful part of the college application process.
  • After financial aid, college fit was the second biggest topic both survey and user interviews participants talked about needing more assistance with.

How might we help users keep track of important dates in a staightforward and manageable way?




Key Takeaways

Due to the difficulty in finding direct competitors, secondary research on admissions today, and the responses from survey and user interview participants, it’s clear that an app like CollegePath is a need. College applicants need a way to keep to:

  • track of important dates
  • get help with the financial aid process
  • find a college that meets their needs and personality

The college application process is also viewed as stressful, expensive, overwhelming and confusing. CollegePath’s mobile app needs to somehow meet these aforementioned needs, while making the college application process feel straightforward and manageable.

Define


User Persona

I created a user persona based on answers received from my user interviews. Calvin is an 18 year old, rising high school senior about to start the college application process. Being the first in his family to both apply and attend college makes Calvin feel apprehensive towards the college application process and all the work it entails.



POV & HMW

Using insights found in my user interviews, I created point of views (POVs) to further delve in Calvin’s needs. I then generated How Might We (HMW) questions from those POVs in order to create a starting point to brainstorm possible solutions from.



Brainstorming

I wrote down potential solutions to the HMW questions I came up with. I tried to continuously come up with new ideas without sticking to just one solution for each question. Some of these ideas were based on features I saw in my competitor analysis, as well as features I wish I saw when exploring those apps.



App Map

I created an app map to outline sections of the app and decide where ‘big features’ such as the Dashboard and College Application Checklist will live.


User Flows

Then I mapped out three main user flows:


  • First-time user wants to see a suggested Application checklist for their Senior year of high school and add some important deadlines for colleges they're applying to from their Common Application account.
  • First-time user wants to find information on financial aid and learn more about how and where to start the financial aid application process.
  • First-time user wants to search for colleges that might be a good fit to add to their college list.

Key Takeaways

Out of the entire define stage, I found it challenging to brainstorm potential solutions. There’s so many real world barriers first-generation students face when applying to college--to the point where it’s hard to see how a mobile app can help.

What helped was reframing my mindset. Instead of thinking about how some barriers cannot be solved with an app, I recognized that my overarching goal is to make the college application process as transparent as possible for the user.

Design


Sketches

Designing an Inclusive Onboarding Process

My research showed how common it is for college-related apps and sites to ask demographic questions related to race, ethnicity and gender identity. This made me wonder if these demographic questions are necessary for my app as well. I decided to include these questions because the information gathered will help connect users to relevant opportunities.

However, this decision lead me to think about a myriad of other considerations. With how prevalent these questions are throughout the college application process, I thought about how these questions, without inclusive answers, might cause a sense of fatigue and frustration for users. I didn't want my onboarding process for this app to contribute to those negative feelings. So I conducted research on how to make these questions more inclusive.

My research showed that three ways to make race/ethnicity and gender identity questions more inclusive:

  • An option for users to enter their own specification to the question, in case an answer relevant to them is not listed.
  • The ability for users to select multiple options. Racial/ethnic identity, as well as gender identity, is complex, and the ability to select multiple answers can help users answer these questions in a way that feels more true to their identity.
  • An explanation why this information is being gathered, along with an option to opt out of question, if desired.

Low-Fidelity Wireframes

I created low-fidelity wireframes that reflect three user flows:

  • The Sign-up and Onboarding process for first-time users.

  • First-time user wants to see a suggested Application checklist for their Senior year of high school and add some important deadlines for colleges they're applying to from their Common Application account.

  • First-time user wants to find information on financial aid and learn more about the different types of aid available.

UI Kit and Branding

After coming up with a couple of names for this app, I decided to name this app CollegePath. I wanted a simple name that clearly indicates that the app helps students find a 'path' to college that is both accessible and straightforward.

To counteract the stress associated with the college application process, I designed CollegePath’s brand as warm, friendly and inviting. I also found that many sites associated with the college application process don’t use much color. Because of that, I used shades of yellow, red, green and blue as brand colors but maintained a muted color palette so it wouldn’t be too overwhelming (or too flashy) for users.

Usability Testing

To test my prototype, I recruited 4 participants: three high school seniors who just completed the college application process, and one current college sophomore. All participants are the first in their family to go to college.

My objectives for usability testing were:

  • Determine if the sign up and onboarding process is comprehensive enough for users to create a robust user profile, without being too overwhelming.
  • Determine if the homepage of the app provides information users need at a glance when they first open the app.
  • Determine if users are able to find the resources they need on the app.
  • Understand how users feel about the Application Checklist feature and see what pain points exist.

I received a lot of positive feedback from participants about the design of the app and how information was organized. All participants said they wished they were able to use an app like this when they were applying for college, especially as first-generation students. This feedback was great to hear and really demonstrated the value of an app.

Some notable comments were:

" I think this app has a lot of value because it's carefully curated and not overwhelming. You can tell you guys are very intentional with what you're doing, which is a positive for me. I would choose you over CollegeBoard. "
" This is a great resource, especially for kids that are first-generation and don’t have access to this information. Adults say to reach out guidance counselors but in a pandemic, that wasn’t easy "
" I think it's really convenient. Instead of looking at the Common Application portal, I’d rather look at this. It’s a hassle to open a new webpage on the Common Application site then check deadlines for schools individually. Here, everything is in one place. "


There was still some areas of concern though. When testing the Application Checklist feature, all participants had trouble marking a task as complete. All participants immediately clicked the plus sign, instead of the correct action: clicking on the task itself. The feedback I received for this feature was that when users hear ‘Checklist’, they assume there will be checkboxes. When they did not see a checkbox, they assumed that the plus sign would act the same way as a checkbox.

Another comment that came up was that during the onboarding process, there isn't an option for users to opt out when asked questions about test scores and GPAs. Some users might not have this information readily available and have to come back to these questions later.


Affinity Maps

While I received a lot of positive feedback from usability testing, I did receive a handful of suggestions on features users would want to see from the CollegePath app. I wanted to map out these comments and see what would be considered a quick fix versus a nice-to-have feature.



Final Iterations

I made changes to the application checklist page, primarily by adding checkboxes instead of a plus sign. I also removed directions because it seemed as though users intuitively know to click the checkbox to mark a task as complete.



Another change is the addition of a task page. Now, when the user taps on the task, it brings the user to the task page with more information, and the ability to turn on reminders and add the task to their phone's calendar.

Next Steps

I definitely would like to conduct another round of usability testing to see how my changes to the Application Checklist feature help users:

  • Mark a task as complete.
  • Turn on notifications for a task.
  • Add a task to their phone’s calendar.

I would also like to explore some of the nice-to-have features such as the ability to favorite a task, and a college search tool.

* * * * *

Final Thoughts


Applying to college is a difficult endeavor for any high school student but it’s especially difficult for first-generation students marginalized by the higher education system. As a former first-generation college student and a former college counselor for other first-generation college students, I feel very strongly about making college accessible to all people. It felt really fulfilling to combine my knowledge and passion for college access to design an app that I know would help students in a very tangible way.
